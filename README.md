# More Than Money: What Life Expectancy and GDP Reveal

## Introduction
This project explores annual GDP and life expectancy across six countries: Chile, China, Germany, Mexico, the United States of America, and Zimbabwe. It also investigates whether higher economic output contributes to longer life expectancy. Summary statistics and categorical/numerical visualisations will be used to solve this problem.

## Background
A nation's economic output, according to the World Bank, is measured by Gross Domestic Product (GDP for short). This metric is measured in US dollars, not adjusted for inflation, and converted to billions to make comparisons easy.
When a baby is born, his or her projected lifespan is known as the life expectancy at birth. Because this metric is influenced by mortality conditions at the time of birth, life expectancy is a popular indicator of a nation's health and economic development.

## Findings and Visualisations
The data was visualised at first using univariate analysis. A bar graph plotting all mentions of each country in the dataset showed all six countries appear the same number of times -- once every year from 2000 to 2015. This suggests that the person who collected this data did not have any patriotic bias. A box plot on life expectancy, meanwhile, indicated that the typical life expectancy in this dataset falls between 70 and 80; Zimbabwe is practically an outlier, seeing as its life expectancies throughout the years were dramatically lower than 70.
Next, bivariate analysis was conducted. A scatterplot demonstrating how Zimbabwe's life expectancy changed with respect to GDP indicates a strong positive relationship. However, this alone does not prove definitively that GDP and lifespan are directly proportional, just that it was the most significant in Zimbabwe. A line graph of China's GDP throughout the years showcases dramatic economic growth after the mid-2000's.
Last, but not least, came multivariate analysis. By plotting each country's life expectancy with time, a trend was found -- each country's life expectancy gradually increased with time. Zimbabwe's dramatic increase in this regard is all the more remarkable despite still being the lowest.

## Key Takeaways
It would be easy to conclude from this analysis that GDP and life expectancy are related. However, the truth is not quite as simple. Chile's and Germany's GDPs may not be as high as that of the United States (especially in the former's case), but Chileans and Germans enjoy higher life expectancies on average. This suggests that wealth is not the only factor in life expectancy. Healthcare, equality, the environment, political stability, and infrastructure may all play important roles.

## Conclusion
Overall, this project shows that GDP and life expectancy are positively related to some degree. One limitation of the data is that only six countries out of over 190 were sampled. In addition, GDP is measured at the national level, not per capita, so viewers have no real idea of how wealth is distributed in each country. Further research could include additional variables -- GDP per capita, healthcare spending, access to medical services, etc. This way the phenomenon of wealthier countries not always ranking high in health can be solved.
